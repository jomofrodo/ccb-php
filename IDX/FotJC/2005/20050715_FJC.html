FLIGHT OF THE JUNGLE CHICKEN: April 2005

On the last mission as we were crossing a river to get back I was filled with joy at the thought of all the people who have made it possible for this work to go on. I wanted to see and thank each of you. We do hope we can and know that finally in heaven we will.

 

Training and Missions: From October 2004 though February 2005 we trained 18 new relief teams inside Burma and went with them to bring help to internally displaced people (IDPs), in the ethnic areas of Burma. During this time the Burma Army attacked the Karen and Karenni people displacing over 8,000 villagers. 

One of the things that struck me most was the positive attitude of the people and their resiliency. They smiled, joked and tried to give us food they had been saving.

A group of five young Karen nurses who traveled with us were also amazing. We had to walk over many mountains and these ladies did this in rubber slippers. It was cold every night and they all huddled together in one group, sharing blankets and living no better than the IDPs.  While moving, we were often very close to SPDC outposts and patrols. One morning I asked the nurses if they were afraid of the Burma Army.  "Yes, we are very afraid", they answered. "But we want to help our people, so we try". The IDPs told us, "We do not put our faith in the world or any nation; we have our faith in God, so we have hope". They keep their belongings packed every day and are ready to flee at a moments notice, but they have not given up.

 

Grace: Before this mission while we were still in a training site for the relief teams, our 2 year old daughter, Suuzanne, became very sick with malaria (pf), which has a high mortality rate especially among infants. The medicine we had did not work. We were in many days by foot and with the Burma Army patrolling the border, all walks out are slow at best. We knew of a medicine that if available could help Suuzanne, but we did not have it. Early in the morning on the third day of her sickness, I woke up at 4:30 and with my flashlight read from Psalms. I read the words, “Be still and know that I am God.” I crawled under the mosquito net where Karen and our two girls were sleeping and prayed for Baby Suu. As I prayed I felt this voice say.” I will take care of her”. I knew it was God and felt peace even though I did not know if that meant He would take care of her in Heaven or here on earth with us. Later that morning a Karen evangelist came walking into our camp with a group of Karen bible school students. They gathered around me and prayed for Baby Suu’s healing. Within 10 minutes of their prayer, a Karen medic walked into the camp and said he heard that baby Suu had a bad form of Malaria and that he had brought the one drug we did not have. He treated her and by the end of the week Baby Suu was up running around like a human cannon ball, very much alive. We know that neither we nor our children will live on this earth forever but we are grateful for every day we do have as a family.

  

GLC Program: While Dave went with the relief teams to the areas under attack, Karen went on a “Good Life Club” mission to villagers and IDPs in an area the Burma Army was not attacking. Her team included, Sahale, Suu , my sister Laurie, Atee and a team of Karen and Karenni medics, teachers and relief workers. They went to reach out to people of all faiths and were able to share together with Karen Christians, Buddhists, and Animists.  

In terms of our program, the premise of the Good Life Club (GLC) comes from Jesus' words in the Bible (John 10:10) "The thief does not come except to steal, and to kill, and to destroy. I have come that they may have life, and that they may have it more abundantly." I believe that Jesus fulfills his promises. All can receive abundant life regardless of their situation.  
In the morning our team sang songs in Karen, Burmese and English followed by a Bible lesson about the life of Ruth using a flannel graph story, emphasizing choices, thankfulness, and being part of the family of God. In the afternoon the team used puppet shows to teach basic health care, village sanitation and personal hygiene. In the evening we used the computer and solar charged batteries to show the Jesus Film in Karen language, and when we had adequate power, The Jungle Book cartoon.

 

Boundaries: For us, God’s boundaries have to do with temptations we are too weak to resist or duties we are not to assume. We may ask God, why can’t I get close to that, or why can’t we do that? God knows our limitations and has a duty for each of us and as we trust and obey we begin to see that God has put our boundaries in pleasant places.
