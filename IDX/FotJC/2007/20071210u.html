Karen’s Christmas Story:
Last night Dave and I discussed this year’s Christmas plans for our family. We are at our jungle training site in the Karen State of Burma where we have conducted a relief mission/leadership training program for the last 4 years. It is October and the beginning of the dry-cool season. This is the best time of the year to move people and supplies both for training and for the relief missions to villagers displaced from their homes by the Burma Army. In this way this will be the seventh year we have spent Christmas and New Years holidays in the jungle.


Despite being war torn communities, the Karen people amaze me with their ‘abundant’ celebrations of Christmas. It actually starts on the last night of November with a celebration called Sweet December. A festive meal, singing, and prayer service, usually around a bonfire at midnight welcomes in the month of December and the Christmas season. Because the Karen were among the first people in Burma to welcome Christ into their hearts, the Christians (over 30% of the Karen are Christian) have a strong history of sharing their faith both among their own people and throughout Burma. Traveling Christmas programs are an integral part of church life and groups pass and meet each other in villages throughout the month of December. In a region devoid of modern transportation it is amazing how far and wide the message of Jesus’ birth is spread in one month on foot through the jungle. Upon the third week of December the festivities reach a new level as whole villages begin to plan their Christmas parties. Unlike my experience of a gathering primarily with family members, the village church prepares for a grand community celebration including multiple church services, breakfast, sports day including prizes and party favors, and an evening program open to not only the whole village but to neighboring villages as well. The most amazing thing is that this ‘Christmas Day’ event doesn’t necessarily happen on December 25th as all the neighboring villages also prepare their own Christmas celebrations and are careful not to overlap with one already planned somewhere else. So, in this corner of the globe where almost every definition of ‘abundance’ has been denied, Christmas is bountiful. But unlike my childhood picture of a tree overflowing with gifts, this scene is overflowing with people and relationships. All day during the celebration, as both Christians and non-Christians stop work, eat, play and rest together, as well as on the subsequent days visiting neighboring villages for their programs. The second of our two jungle homes is in the largest of a group of three villages. We have typically celebrated Christmas there four times each year including our own western family version. The treasure of this time is certainly not in goods that they can give to each other, but in time and presence which is quite similar to the original gift of Jesus’ birth to the world.


And yet, ironically, despite having experienced hearty Karen Christmases during the past few years I was feeling more and more depressed as I realized that due to the timing of the relief training and follow-on missions starting in mid-December and the length of travel time to and from the hide-sites to help IDPs, the children and I would be separated from Dave on Christmas Day, December 25th. In fact, none of us would even be at our own house as the children and I would still be on our Good Life Club School Tour, traveling among small villages and displaced persons sites. This is always a good and fruitful time, but not the special or ‘set-apart’ atmosphere I looked forward to at Christmas. So, despite all the ‘abundance' I knew in my mind about the Karen celebration, I had a hard time swallowing the pill of how Christmas was going to look this year. The cultural trappings of decorations, special food, a tree, holiday music, scented candles, etc. and most significant, being together as a family were so special that I did my best to continue them even at our jungle home, as well as participating in the village festivities. But last night the thought of arriving at December 25th without any of these things was quite painful. It felt like something dear was being torn away and all the joy stored up for that day would be lost.


And yet, as time passed, I was strangely comforted by a basic truth that seemed to hold power enough to turn the tide of my emotions. Christmas is the celebration of the birth of Jesus and in all honesty, I don’t need special smells, tastes, sounds, decorations or even members of my family to welcome the birth of a child. I have celebrated that ‘Jesus is the reason for the season’ every year of my life yet never without any accompanying traditions. So now, what if this year, on Christmas day, the birth of Jesus is truly all we have to think about? Is it special enough to be a joyful celebration all by itself? This was a striking thought and in God’s deep way He spoke to me saying, “It is the core of your faith, and you will see, it is enough”. I felt ashamed at my great dependence on the temporal to add meaning to the divine and thankful for God’s grace in my lack of appreciation for the pure gift of His Son.


In all truth, even after the revelation of the night, I still woke up the following morning to a gray cloud in my mind’s eye about the Christmas conversation and remembered that I needed to write this article about ‘Christmas in the Jungle’. I had previously started writing on a different theme, a less personal comparison between Eastern and Western celebrations, but the conviction to give something ‘real’ and genuine was stronger, and despite opening the window for all to read about my spiritual shortcomings, I felt there was greater power in sharing the truth. Now, several hours and one Christmas story finished, I can joyfully testify that in describing how the Karen communities fluidly move the date of their celebrations around I am content to celebrate our family Christmas on New Years’ Day and to let Jesus come, by Himself, as the gift, on His birthday, December 25th. As I have experienced that Emmanuel comes to us every day of the year, in all our joys and trials, may your Christmas be filled with his abundant power and love. God is with us! Merry Christmas and Happy New Year!
Love, Karen
