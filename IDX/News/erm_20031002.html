MISSION PURPOSE:
To bring emergency relief, hope, love and compassion to IDPs and villagers in the fighting area of Mae Plae To, Paan District, Karen State, Burma in September/October 2003.  Distribution of aid, presents and medicine provided by the Free Burma Rangers. 
SITUATION SUMMARY:
Currently the SPDC and the DKBA are carrying out a military operation code-named "Power Over the Land" in order to occupy and control this area and to destroy the No 7 Brigade Headquarters.  The operation began in the last week of July and continues to the present day.  Due to this offensive over 500 people were displaced and more than 40 families fled to Thailand in September.  When fleeing they had to abandon their homes, farms, cattle and belongings.  Although most of the IDPs were able to flee, one 19 year-old pregnant woman was shot and killed by the SPDC and one man stepped on a landmine. 
The Mae Ple Doe area has been split into three sections.  This has resulted in problems for  the following villages: Toe Thu Khi village, Day Law Phya Village, Mae Ple Wah Khi village, Thay Wah Bu Village, Wah Klu Bu village, Bu Way village, Htee Wah Kaley Village and Pan Klu village. Some have been able to escape to Thailand, some are living in the jungle, and others are being used by the SPDC and the DKBA to carry out forced labor and to porter for the troops.  Transportation routes to the east have been blocked by landmines, which were laid by the SPDC and the DKBA.  The SPDC and the DKBA are fighting to gain control of the mountainous region to the west of Moei river.  The are conducting a three pronged attack, with troops advancing from the north, south and west.  There are nine SPDC and three DKBA Battalions involved in this operation. IB 339, LIB 705, and 1 Battalion from DKBA 999 attacking from the north vicinity Htee Nu Hta (across from Mae La refugee camp). LIB 702, LIB 706, and 1 Battalion of DKBA 999 attacking from the south vicinity Mae Plae Do. IB 97, LIB 709, and the special battalion of DKBA 999 attacking from the west vicinity Lay Gau (across from Mae Ramat). Three more battalions , LIB 703, LIB 708, and LIB 709 were sent in to reinforce this offensive on 4 October 2003. They are using heavy artillery including 120mm, 81mm and 60mm 75 launchers, machine guns and landmines.  Homes, farms and animals owned by civilians in the area are being destroyed.   
MEDICAL TREATMENT
During the mission, medical treatment was given to over 200 villagers living in make-shift tents and in the jungle.  The main problems encountered were malaria, ERI, skin infections, dysentery and general stress due to the situation and the living conditions.  Medical help was provided to a mother who gave birth to her child 14 days before whilst living in the jungle.  Both mother and baby are ok at present.  Many elderly people were seen by the medic and they were given vitamins and other medicine.  All excess medicinal supplies were given to the backpack medics in the area, as they had used all their supplies.  This will enable them to continue to provide medical treatment to the people in the area.
AREA DESCRIBED
The Mae Ple Doe area, which is 25 km from Mae La, on the Thai border, stretches from the Kaw Kareik and Myawaddy townships in the south, deep into the jungle in the north. It spans the Mae Ple river.   The main transport route in the area is a road which runs from Kaw Kareik township to Mae La via the Thay Wah Bu Village.  The area covers around 25 - 30 square km.  Approximately 60% of this is jungle and much is mountainous.         
Daily Mission Report:
26 September 2003The 15 person relief team departed to the first IDP site.
27 September 2003The team walked for 6 hours to the first IDP site.  A meeting was held with the Karen leaders and a religious service was held.
28 September 2003The team walked to Thay Ner Oou village and immediately set up a medical clinic for the villagers.  Many villagers were suffering from malnutrition as their staple diet at present is rice and salt.  Support and compassion were given to the villagers and a religious ceremony was held for them.  The team interviewed 2 IDPs.
Interview  1Name: Naw Mu ChoFemale from Thay Wah Bu Village, 30 years old, religion: Animist
She had to flee Thay Wah Bu Village due to heavy shelling by the SPDC and DKBA.  She wants to return to her home to harvest the rice and live in her home again but she is unable due to the many landmines that have now been planted in the area.
She is unsure where she can go now and is considering fleeing to Thailand.
Interview 2Name:  Naw Khin NyainFemale from Htee Wah Kaley Village, 40 years old, 5 children, religion: Buddhist.
She said that on the 22 September 2003 there was fighting close to the Htee Wah Kaley Village between the KNLA and the SPDC.  When the fighting ceased the SPDC and DKBA began shooting into the village.  While running away from the troops her pregnant, 19 year old daughter Naw Paw was caught in the gunfire and was killed.  The other villagers managed to flee in what they were wearing but were unable to take any clothes or belongings with them.  Her husband has gone to Thailand to buy rice.  She is distraught about the death of her daughter and the loss of their home.
Following these interviews the team walked for a further hour to another IDP site known as Lay Wah Khee.  A medical clinic was again set up immediately.  Clothes and aid were distributed along with compassion and caring words.    At this time the fighting was very close by, around 1km away and the IDPs were frightened and afraid.  The team interviewed 1 IDP.
Interview 3Name:  Naw May OwnFemale, from Thay Wah Bu Village, 18 years old, 2 week old baby boy, religion Animist
She said that on 7 September 2003 the SPDC and DKBA entered Thay Wah Bu Village during the night time and began shooting indiscriminately.   At that time her baby was 5 days old and she managed to escape with him into the jungle.  Her mother-in-law was shot but also managed to escape.  She is alone at the moment as her husband has taken his mother to Thailand to receive medical assistance at Mae La refugee camp.  She is feeling very weak but she cannot return to her village to get food as she is too afraid.  
The team walked to a further IDP site known as Mae Taw Htaw Khe.  This was during the night so a night service was held and then 1 IDP was interviewed.
Interview 4Name:  Saw Dee KloMale, from Thay Wah Bu Village, 30 years old, married with 3 children, religion Buddhist.
He said that Maw Hla Wah, the local leader of the DKBA, entered Thay Wah Bu Village and demanded from the villagers chickens, goats, rice, a pig and other belongings.  He took these without offering any money. 
He also told us that in Htee Wah Klay village, on 22 September 2003, the same DKBA group, led by Maw Hla Wah, attempted to rape two female villagers, Naw Na Ree (16 years old) and Naw Khin Than (19 years old).  They were assaulted but managed to escape.  The troops then burned down 5 houses and stole 2 pigs and all the chickens belonging to the villagers.
29 September 2003Beginning early in the morning the team walked to 3 different IDP sites, arriving at the first at around 0830.  At each site a medical clinic was immediately set up to treat any medical problems.  Gifts and clothes were distributed and religious services were held.  Many villagers told of problems they had encountered, the most common being loss of cattle and homes due to shelling and machine gun fire.  They are all too afraid to return to their villages.  4 IDPs were individually interviewed.
Interview 5Name:  Saw Dah PoeMale, from Wah Klu Bu village, 37 years old, religion Buddhist.
He said that due to the fighting they had to close the village school and flee.  Some villagers are hiding in the jungle and some have fled to Thailand.  They do not know if their village is still standing and what has become of their belongings.  They are too afraid to return.
Interview 6Name:  Naw Mu ChuFemale, from Thee Wah Bu village, 60 years old
She told us that she fled her village on 7 September 2003 because of fighting in the area.  Since then she has been living in the jungle.  She does not know if she will ever return to her village as she is too scared.  She does not know what has happened to her house and belongings.  She is afraid and distraught.
Interview 7Name:  U Than HtunMale, from Thee Wah Bu village, 45 years old, married, religion Buddhist
He said he fled from his village due to the SPDC and DKBA  shelling.  He is afraid that he will be captured and forced to porter for their troops.  He has lost all his belongings including buffalo and cows.  He is hiding in the jungle to avoid the forced recruitment and is unable to return home.
Interview 8Name:  Naw Dae GayFemale, from Thee Wah Bu village, 50 years old, married with 9 children and grandchildren, religion Animist.
She fled Thee Wah Bu village on 7 September 2003 because of shelling and shooting around the village.  She has no belongings or clothes with her.  She was forced to leave her house, chickens and other belongings in the village and is too afraid to return.  She is unsure where to go next and is considering escaping to Thailand if the situation worsens.
30 September 2003The team walked to Ta Doh Khi Hta village.  A medical clinic was again set up immediately and patients were treated.  A church service was held for the IDPs. Medicines and aid were distributed. Following this, at around 1pm, the team arrived at Wah Klu Bu village and interviewed the following IDP.
Interview 9Name:  Saw Kyaw DooMale, from Wah Klu Bu village, 10 years old.
The boy told us that he heard the SPDC shooting in his village so along with his family he fled into the jungle.  Afterwards he accompanied his father back to the village to take care of the cattle.  He is a 3 standard student but his school has been closed for 3 months.  He would like to study, but believes it is his duty to follow his father. 
The team walked back to the camp area they had used on the first night.  At this point the shelling was around 500 meters away.   Despite this the Karen leaders had stayed at the same place.  A meeting was held with Karen soldiers and then a religious night service was held.  Before departure the team left all excess medical and miscellaneous supplies with the backpack medics and the Karen soldiers.
01 October 2003The goal of the team had been to visit and give material and spiritual help to the IDPs .  As this goal had now been achieved they left the area to return to the border.  During the return trip the team visited a Karen Army leader to get information about fighting in the area and to decide a safe route to travel.  They helped to carry supplies to the Army camp.  Upon arrival the SPDC were shelling heavily so the team took refuge in the shell bunkers until it the shelling reduced in ferocity and it was safe to continue.  By the end of the day the team made it safely to No 7 Brigade HQ.
02 October 2003Following a debriefing by a KNLA General where the team described the situation they had seen, they left and returned home.  In the evening an evaluation was held regarding the accomplishments and problems with the mission.  The following was decided:
EVALUATION DECISIONS
  A video camera with a solar powered recharging unit would be extremely useful for the team in order to be able to accurately report the plight of the IDPs.
 More equipment should be taken on the next mission for the porters that help to carry the supplies.
 Walkie Talkies should be taken on the next mission to enable the team to remain in contact  at all times.
A small tape recorder should be taken on the next mission to record interviews with IDPs.
  More medical equipment is needed in the IDP and  should be taken next time.
 Barbers shears should be taken on the next mission.
