8.8.88

From the People....

August 8, 1988 (8-8-88) is a special date for the Burmese student movement. For several months anti-government demonstrations calling for an end to 26 years of one-party rule had been growing throughout the country. On August 8, these demonstrations began to reach a peak as a general strike was called all over the country and tens of thousands of Burmese people, young, old, men and women, swarmed into the streets calling for President Sein Lwin to step down and restore democracy through free elections. In Rangoon, the people marched 25 abreast through the wide tree-lined streets. Their procession stretched for 3 kilometers and was made up of students, government workers, medical staff, and laborers. The mood was that of a celebration even though in some parts of the country demonstrators were being killed. Perhaps as many as 360 people died on this one day.

Day after day the demonstrations continued, and the number of people participating grew. Even some military officers joined in to show solidarity with the people. It seemed to the students that victory was certain. Then on September 18, Gen. Saw Maung staged a military coup, and began a brutal repression against the democratic movement. All over the country there were reports of demonstrators being shot or bayoneted. Thousands of people were arrested and put in prison. Many of them were never seen again. Some people estimate that at least 8,000 people were killed during these demonstrations. The majority of them were unarmed students and children.

For the students, hope for an end to the one-party system of Ne Win through peaceful means ended. Thousands of them began the long trek to the Thai-Burma border area where they could seek refuge with some of the insurgent groups there. Although the international press covered these events in great detail for several months, their interest soon faded as other international stories became more sensational. Now, the world is slowly forgetting Burma, the struggle of the Burmese people and their plea for the right to participate in their own government
