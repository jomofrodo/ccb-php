Internally Displaced People of Burma

A tragedy that can be stopped...

 

Due to the oppression of the dictators of Burma there are approximately 2million people inside Burma who have been displaced from their homes. For most displaced ethnic Burmans, (about 1 million), the dictatorship has relocated them by force or threat of force in order to make more land available for the regime’s business projects. Most of these displaced live in or near large urban centers firmly under the regime’s control

For the ethnic minorities the situation is much worse. In the minority areas such as the Karen, Karenni and Shan states, up to 1 million people have been forced to leave their homes and live in relocation sites were their freedom is severely restricted and their treatment brutal. In the minority areas the dictatorship has forced people to leave their villages with only what they can carry, they are then forced to move to a relocation site. The villagers’ livestock are usually confiscated and their villages burned to the ground. Many areas are then mined to prevent villagers from returning. In some cases the villagers are not given a choice of going to a concentration site, but are attacked by the Burma army, their villages burned, and their people murdered or chased into the jungle.

The regime’s stated purpose is to "crush all resistance" and to" punish" the ethnic groups. The result of this scorched-earth policy is that many have fled either their homes or the brutal conditions of the relocation sites. These people (for example in the Karen state, over 200,000), are currently hiding in the jungle along the Thai-Burma border. They are in need of security, medical care, clothing , school supplies, moral encouragement and spiritual support.

In response to this ongoing emergency, the Ethnic groups have organized teams of medics, teachers, and church workers to send relief to the Internally Displaced Persons.

Teams carry rice, medicine, and educational supplies to try to meet the IDP’s immediate needs. The teams

minister to all IDPs, regardless of race, religion, or political affiliation. On one team, a pastor reported that, "all the people were glad to see us but were very afraid to stay and listen to us, so we told them ‘don’t be afraid, we are not soldiers or politicians, we are angels sent from God to help you. God loves you and wants to help you. You can’t depend on the foreigners, finally you can not depend on us, you can only depend on God!’ "

The IDPs are grateful for any help and one old man said, "Thank you for caring, we know the world has not forgotten us. Please come back soon." While the IDPs asked for more help especially for IDPs deeper inside Burma, they were terrified of being discovered by dictatorship troops (State Peace and Development Council) or to be later discovered as having been in contact with outside supporters. This atmosphere of terror was due to the previous experiences of these IDPs. In this area SPDC troops have systematically burned villages, murdered villagers, raped and tortured them, and slaughtered their livestock. In cases where villages are not destroyed, the villagers live under the constant scrutiny of SPDC troops and are subject to forced labor and random punishment.

The IDP teams have found that Malaria, Typhoid, dysentery, and malnutrition are widespread. Land mine and shrapnel victims were also found in many areas. The teams treat all the patients they can, but demands soon outstrip medical supplies. The teams also find it difficult to follow-up on patients as they often had to move due to SPDC patrols.

On another mission, one team reported that even in villages they passed through, where the people still remained, life there was almost intolerable. For example, in village X a notice had just been sent to the village headman from the local SPDC Army outpost. It demanded 3 men for forced labor each day, 2 large baskets of rice and one message even added that, "we heard Mr. Y shot a barking deer, why didn’t he give some of it to us?" These types of demands may not make the headlines like the SPDC’s murder, rape, and torture of civilians, but it is this type of oppression that is most pervasive.

What makes the mistreatment of the citizens of Burma even more demoralizing is the International community’s lack of will to take strong measures to stop the oppression. It is the hope of the people of Burma that they can solve their own problems, yet they ask for help as so far, their attempts at a peaceful and just solution have been rejected by the SPDC. The IDPs are the most recent evidence of this and they need our help, not only in the provision of relief, but in assisting them to change the situation in Burma. One way the international community can help is to apply enough pressure to the dictatorship until they’re willing to change, and in the meantime provide direct relief and security to IDPs inside Burma.
