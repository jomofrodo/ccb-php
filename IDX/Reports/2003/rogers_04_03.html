Summary

The ethnic minorities in Burma are “living in constant fear; fear of when the Burmese are going to knock on the door,” and the situation in Burma is at a “critical point,” according to the General Secretary of the National Democratic Front (NDF), Zing Cung. No progress has been made in political reform or human rights, and therefore much greater international pressure is required. “The United Nations has passed resolutions every year since 1994, calling for tripartite dialogue and condemning human rights abuses, but it has taken no significant steps,” Zing Cung said. “The UN and the international community must take stronger measures.” One problem is the differences in approach between the European Union (EU) and the Association of South East Asian Nations (ASEAN). The EU has taken a strong stand with sanctions, but ASEAN, especially China and India, have practised “constructive engagement”. There needs to be consistency. “The international community needs one voice, a voice for [greater] diplomatic pressure and sanctions.”

 

While the release of Dr Salai Tun Than and 21 other prisoners of conscience on May 4th should be welcomed, it does not signal significant reform. Widespread systematic gross violations of human rights, including extrajudicial killings, the use of forced labour, the use of child soldiers, rape, looting, extortion and suppression of political and religious freedom continue to be carried out by the ruling military junta, the State Peace and Development Council (SPDC)[1], in Burma today, particularly among the ethnic minorities such as the Karen, Karenni, Shan and Chin.

 

No progress has been made on political reform since the release from house arrest of National League for Democracy (NLD) leader Daw Aung San Suu Kyi a year ago. Although her own freedom of movement is to be welcomed, the SPDC attempts to restrict people from meeting with her or attending rallies. Dialogue between the SPDC and NLD has stalled, and the regime appears to have rejected Thailand’s offer to mediate between the SPDC and the ethnic groups. Although there have been some signs that the SPDC is interested in talking with the Karen National Union (KNU), the SPDC’s pre-condition for talks, that the KNU surrender their arms prior to engaging in dialogue, is unacceptable to the KNU. The Karen rightly expect meaningful assurances that dialogue will lead to progress and that the SPDC will cease carrying out further human rights violations before they can surrender their arms. Evidence from those ethnic groups, such as the Kachin, which have signed ceasefire agreements is not encouraging – even after agreeing a ceasefire, the SPDC has continued to carry out human rights abuses.

 

This report documents a number of human rights violations, including the use of child soldiers, and extortion of food and alcohol from villagers by SPDC soldiers. Christian Solidarity Worldwide (CSW) interviewed a former child soldier who defected from the Burma Army and is being looked after by the All Burma Students Democratic Front (ABSDF), and two child soldiers who are now in the care of the KNU (see page 13). This is in addition to another former Burmese child soldier interviewed by CSW in November 2002 (see CSW Report, Visit to The Thai-Burmese Border, November 2002).

 

CSW received copies of letters from SPDC commanders to villagers in Karen and Karenni states[2]. One letter demands that they immediately provide alcohol, chickens and other supplies and threatens that “if you are absent you will be punished severely”; another letter orders the villagers to arrest people in their area who have fled forced labour and return them to “the labouring sites”.

 

This evidence is yet more confirmation of the reports of human rights violations including the use of forced labour and child soldiers provided by CSW and numerous other organisations including the International Labour Organisation (ILO), Human Rights Watch[3], Amnesty International, the Karen Human Rights Organisation, the Shan Womens Action Network, the Free Burma Rangers and Burma Issues, over many years.

 

The optimism for imminent progress which Daw Aung San Suu Kyi’s release last year generated has disappeared. The mood among democracy activists and ethnic minorities appears to be more pessimistic about prospects for immediate change, although they maintain their hope for the longer-term vision of democracy and freedom. “No change” is the phrase several people used in describing the current situation.

 

CSW heard reports, not yet verified by CSW, of growing militarization in Burma. Specifically, CSW was informed that last year Russia supplied Burma with 12 Mig 29 fighter planes, Israel provided laser-guided bombs and Russia supplied a nuclear reactor based south of Mandalay. It was also reported that SPDC army officers have been called for chemical warfare training, and that 300 Burmese are currently in Moscow being trained in nuclear physics. Furthermore, there are signs that Mossad is involved in training Burmese military intelligence east of Mandalay. It should be noted that Burma is reportedly one of the richest sources of uranium in the world. It must be emphasised, however, that CSW has not verified these reports, although they came from credible sources. Nevertheless, the international community should investigate these and other reports of increasing militarization.

It was reported during CSW’s visit that the United Kingdom Government has recently agreed to donate US$281,600 to Burma for environmental projects, including US$192,000 for a bat conservation survey in Karen and Mon states to be carried out by the Harrison Institute in Britain and Rangoon University[4]. At the same time, hundreds of thousands of IDPs in Karen state, and over a million IDPs in total, receive almost no humanitarian assistance from the British Government. CSW would question the British Government’s aid priorities.

 

The only way to break the deadlock is for the international community to significantly and severely increase and intensify political, diplomatic and economic pressure on the SPDC. There is a unanimous view among ethnic minority and pro-democracy groups that trade and investment sanctions should be increased. According to a Shan spokesman speaking to CSW, economic investment and trade only benefits the regime. The people do not gain any benefit. Indeed, the regime spends an estimated 40 per cent of its annual budget on the military and only 2 per cent on health, so foreign investment is indirectly assisting the SPDC’s military offensive against the ethnic minorities. Although sanctions may harm the people, the people are suffering anyway – and sanctions at least, if they are applied effectively, will strip the SPDC of its financial resources and force it to enter into dialogue.

 

While there is unanimity in regard to sanctions, there are some differences of opinion over whether military intervention should be threatened by the international community. However, among ethnic minority representatives the majority view seemed to be in favour of military action. This is an option that the United Nations, and the United States, United Kingdom and the European Union should at least consider.

 

1.      Introduction

Christian Solidarity Worldwide (CSW) has visited the Karen and Karenni, and more recently the Shan, on the Thai-Burmese border many times since 1989[5]. CSW is an international, inter-denominational Christian human rights organisation, focused on documenting abuses of human rights including religious freedom, and using that information for advocacy campaigns. CSW also provides humanitarian assistance in the form of medical expertise and financial support for several educational and medical projects, both in the refugee camps in Thailand and among the Internally Displaced People (IDPs) in Burma. CSW has representatives in the United Kingdom, the United States, Australia, Hong Kong, Canada, India and Scandinavia.

 

On this visit, CSW went to refugee camps around Mae Sariang, Mae Sot and Mae Hong Son, met with IDPs in Karen and Shan states in Burma, provided medical assistance and training in Karen areas, and met with representatives of the All Burma Students Democratic Front (ABSDF), the Karen National Union (KNU), the Shan State Army (SSA) and the Restoration Council of the Shan State (RCSS), the Karenni National Progressive Party (KNPP), the Chin National Front (CNF), the National Democratic Front (NDF), as well as Non-Governmental Organisations (NGOs) such as the Burmese Border Consortium (BBC), Burma Issues, the Free Burma Rangers and several other NGOs in Bangkok, Chiang Mai, Mae Sariang, Mae Sot and Mae Hong Son.

 

2.      Purposes of visit

CSW undertakes several visits each year to the Thai-Burmese border. This visit had the following objectives:

 

a)      To obtain further information and evidence of the human rights and humanitarian situation facing the Karen, Karenni, Shan and other ethnic minorities in Burma and on the Thai-Burma border;

b)      To visit existing CSW-supported projects;

c)      To deliver funds donated by supporters in Hong Kong;

d)      To maintain and develop relationships with the Karen, Karenni and Shan, and with other organisations working with them;

e)      To identify new needs and channels for aid and advocacy information, and develop new relationships;

f)       To provide medical training and assistance to the Karen;

g)      To encourage and stand in solidarity with the refugees and IDPs in Burma

6.      Summary of Meetings

 

23rd April – Burma Issues presentation on Internally Displaced People (IDPs) at the Foreign Correspondents Club of Thailand, Bangkok

Burma Issues presented a new film called No Place To Go, highlighting the plight of IDPs in Burma. According to Burma Issues, there are 110,000 registered refugees (and at least 140,000 refugees in total) in Thailand, but there are at least one million IDPs inside Burma (270,000 living in the jungles; 365,000 in forced relocation camps). The Burma Army has pursued a policy of “four cuts” for many years, aimed at cutting ethnic minority resistance groups’ access to food, funds, recruits and intelligence. One man who has been on the run for several years said on camera: “I just want to live in one place, my own home”. The Burma Army uses villagers as porters to carry ammunition for them, deploys forced labour to construct roads and takes women to “entertain” soldiers in the front-line. In total, 2,350 villages have been destroyed by the Burma Army, and there are 176 relocation camps. It was noted that Thailand is not a signatory to the International Convention on Refugees, so the refugees in Thailand are regarded as temporarily displaced people and are thus denied some of the legal security and protection to which they would be entitled under the International Convention. It was also emphasised that the primary need for IDPs is an improvement in the security situation in Burma. The first step would be for the SPDC to admit that there are IDPs (a fact that it currently denies).

 

Following this presentation, CSW’s international consultant Benedict Rogers was interviewed by Radio Free Asia about forced labour in Shan state and the use of child soldiers.

 

24th-28th April – Meetings in Chiang Mai with NGOs and missionaries, and visit to Asian Tribal Ministries base

 

26th April – Meeting with Kho Than Khe, Chairman, All Burma Students Democratic Front (ABSDF), Mae Sariang

Kho Than Khe expressed support for the Karen National Union (KNU)’s political struggle and its efforts for reconciliation, and paid tribute to the KNU and the Karenni National Progressive Party (KNPP) for their sacrifice in this struggle. “They have sacrificed more than any other groups in Burma,” he said. They are “fighting for their dignity” and trying to resist the SPDC’s efforts to destroy their human rights. ABSDF has had good co-operation with the KNU and KNPP since the ABSDF was formed by students who had fled to the Thai-Burmese border after the uprising in 1988. The KNU has played an important leadership role among the resistance groups operating in the border areas. The organisations working on the Thai border serve an important role: without them, it would be very difficult for foreign Non-Governmental Organisations (NGOs) to gain information and provide assistance. But ABSDF is experiencing some financial difficulties at the moment as a result of the decision by some foreign NGOs, such as USAID and International Rescue Committee (IRC), to cease provision of humanitarian support to ABSDF. This decision was reportedly made because ABSDF is engaged in armed struggle. IRC ceased providing humanitarian assistance in December 2001. ABSDF receives some assistance from Burmese friends abroad, and also receives medicines from the National Health and Education Committee (NHEC) and Medical Mercy Canada. The Norwegian Burma Council provides support for schools and for movements such as mass education and primary healthcare. The Danish Burma Council funds training and documentation and research work. But no one is providing food.

In terms of human rights violations, there is, according to Kho Than Khe, “no change”. The use of forced labour, child soldiers, rape and looting continues. He said that if Burma does gain democracy one day, there will be a lot of work to do in educating people, especially the military and former military, in values of human rights and respect. The Burma Army, he added, “does not understand how to live healthy lives, spiritually and physically”. There is “no law, no regulation, no education”.

Burma Army troops sometimes carry out bizarre attacks on livestock when they enter a village, in order to cut opposition groups’ food supplies. According to Kho Than Khe, troops have been known to kill the cock chicken by stabbing its eyes, thus making it blind. The cock then wanders around blinded and falls into the village well – thus destroying the village cock and contaminating the water supply. In another instance, troops nailed pigs on their nose causing immense pain and a slow death. Soldiers often eat villagers’ animals, and when they don’t eat them they destroy them.

The SPDC soldiers plant landmines around their operational areas. If a villager stands on a landmine, they are likely to lose limbs or even lives. But the SPDC troops, regardless of injury or death caused, demand compensation from the villagers for the landmines that have been stepped on.

The ABSDF provided CSW with copies of three letters written by Burma Army Column Commanders to village heads in Karen state, which extort goods and demand the return of forced porters who have escaped. The reference to porters means that the SPDC cannot therefore claim (as they do) to have stopped forced portering. Copies of these with a full translation are available from CSW. For security reasons the names of the villages have been removed. In summary, they make the following demands:

 

Ø      Letter A details a report of three porters who escaped from a Burma Army column and headed in the direction of the village. They are hiding in the jungle in the “slash and burn” areas. The letter orders the village headman to find the escaped porters, arrest them and return them to the column.

Ø      Letter B urges the village head to come to the monastery nearby as soon as he has received the letter, and to bring two bottles of alcohol, one chicken and three packs of cheroots. “If you are absent you will be punished severely”.

Ø      Letter C orders a village head to arrest some porters who have escaped.

 

Greater international pressure on the SPDC is required, according to Kho Than Khe. “Without pressure, the SPDC cannot enter into dialogue. They are playing at it – at the moment the ball is in their legs,” he said. All groups, ethnic and democratic resistance organisations, ceasefire forces and non-ceasefire groups, want dialogue. “No one wants to continue fighting. We are all tired.” The KNU has been fighting for 50 years; the ABSDF has been fighting for 15 years. “Even 15 years is not a short time. It is a long time in our lives. We don’t know how long we have to continue living in the remote areas, struggling with hardship lives. The international community should put pressure on the SPDC to enter dialogue.”

            The economic situation inside Burma is worsening day by day and the SPDC is losing control of the economy. There is a banking crisis, inflation and a collapse in the currency exchange system. “People are frustrated,” Kho Than Khe said. This frustration has led to some individual solo demonstrations in front of the city hall in Rangoon, a recent protest by students and Buddhist monks in front of the British Embassy in Rangoon, and the hunger strike by Christian prisoner of conscience Dr Salai Tun Than. “People dare to speak more openly than before.” In some areas there is such a shortage of rice that women and children stop cars in the streets and ask for rice and money. “They are craving food,” he explained. “Once Burma was the rice bowl of Asia; now Burmese people are craving rice. They don’t have anything to eat.” This frustration, he added, will get bigger each day until it “erupts”. But it needs to be organised systematically as a political movement. “If the people power movement is strong we can overthrow the regime immediately.”

            Burma is a multi-ethnic nation with different visions and ideas among the various groups. But what unites all peoples of Burma is a common vision to topple the regime, establish peace, democracy and a federal union. “And everyone can agree to sanctions,” he said.

            We discussed the recent campaign in Britain, led by the Burma Campaign and CSW, to persuade British-American Tobacco (BAT) to withdraw from its investments in Burma. One of BAT’s defences for its decision to remain in Burma until now has been the idea that jobs would be lost if it pulled out. Kho Than Khe rejects this view. “Companies are exploiting Burma, exploiting the cheap labour, and they only care about their own interests. Who will gain from the investments? Only a tiny amount of the money will go to the people. If we encourage investment democracy cannot survive, and military rule will last a long time. All people are suffering – companies withdrawing from Burma will not increase the suffering [and it might lead to a resolution].”

 

Visit to Mae Kong Kha (Sala) Camp

The camp has 16,000 refugees. The living conditions in the area of the camp CSW visited seemed to be poorer than some other camps along the Thai-Burmese border.

 

Discussion with Wai Linn Zinn, Joint General Secretary of ABSDF

The SPDC uses child soldiers in order to enforce its dictatorship and build up its military forces to its goal of 500,000 troops. Many child soldiers are so young that their weapons are too heavy to carry.

 

Meeting with Khin Kyaw, an ABSDF commander

Khin Kyaw said that out of the 50 million people in Burma, over 40 million support the democracy and resistance movements. After the 1990 elections, in which the NLD led by Daw Aung San Suu Kyi won 80% of the parliamentary seats, the SPDC army personnel did not want to fight, and many of them had voted for NLD. The SPDC commits many “management errors”. Junior soldiers are very tired and depressed and do not want to fight any more, but they cannot refuse orders. SPDC officers abuse their subordinates. SPDC privates are paid just 8,000 kyats a month, but a basket of rice costs 10,000 kyats. Corporals are paid 12,000 kyats, captains 20,000 kyats. Soldiers who want to leave the army must find at least three new soldiers before they can be discharged.

             The use of child soldiers only started in 1988. Prior to 1988, people below the age of 18 did not join the army, Khin Kyaw said. After 1988 SPDC felt the need to increase the size of its army. Before 1988, there were 10 brigades and 10 divisions, now there are 40 brigades and 12 divisions.

 

Meeting with Maw Lay, Adjutant Officer of KNU 102 Battalion under 5th Brigade

Maw Lay has been in the KNU since 1973. He is on the front-line frequently, most recently returning from the front-line on April 5th. He said that Way Ghy was attacked by SPDC troops last month and there is some movement in the area south of Deh Bo No.

 

30th April – Meeting with Saw Tamlawah and Lydia Tamlawah, Maesot

Saw Tamlawah co-ordinates the Veterans’ Association and his wife Lydia runs the Karen Womens Organisation. Saw Tamlawah said that many Karen people “feel they have been treated unfairly after what they did for the British during and after the Second World War”. Many Karen gave their lives out of loyalty to the British and the Allies, but Britain has done little to help the Karen since then. Britain has not given any pensions or compensation to Karen war veterans, although the British War Veterans have collected some funds to give to Karen veterans.

 

 

Meeting with Padoh Mahn Sha, General Secretary of the KNU

Mahn Sha is Po Karen from the Delta region, and is a Buddhist. He has been the General Secretary of the KNU since 2000, and was previously personal secretary to former KNU President (now Vice-President and Minister of Defence) General Saw Bo Mya. This in itself demonstrates that the KNU is not simply a Christian movement dominated by Hill Karens, an accusation that is often made. He joined the Karen struggle in 1966, and for the first eight years worked in the KNU delegation to the ethnic nationalities’ alliance, travelling to Karenni, Pa-O, Shan and Kachin areas.

The creation of the breakaway Democratic Karen Buddhist Army (DKBA) in January 1995 is said to have been caused partly by the lack of representation of Buddhists among the mainly Christian-dominated KNU leadership. However, Mahn Sha, a Buddhist, disagrees. “Some people say it is a Christian revolution, but it is not. There are many Buddhists in the KNU. The majority of soldiers are Buddhist. It is very wrong to say it is a Christian revolution – it is only a Karen revolution,” he said. When some Buddhists in the KNU started agitating and demonstrating, he went to try to talk with them and they detained him for eight days. “The DKBA problem came from the military regime. They are trying to divide our revolution,” he said. He acknowledged that some Buddhists among the Karen were “very tired in the frontline and very weak in politics”, but added that the Burmese regime “took the chance to create the DKBA and take advantage of Karen weak points”. The DKBA was, Mahn Sha said, “created by the SPDC” and is still working with the SPDC, especially under the control of Military Intelligence (MI 25). The DKBA has in recent years become involved in narcotics and border trade, and many DKBA leaders are “very rich” while their troops are very poor.

The KNU is funded through a taxation system on small-scale border trade in livestock and other commodities, in which the KNU takes 4% from traders. But compared with Karen-controlled trade 30 years ago, the trade now is very limited. The KNU also has a 4% tax on people in KNU-controlled areas, and the KNU discusses with Karen villagers how they can contribute to the struggle.

Earlier this year the KNU destroyed a gas pipeline three times: on February 1st in Lamine area in Mon state; on March 29th near Myangalay, near Pa’an, Karen state; and on April 15th, in Mon state again. According to Mahn Sha, the KNU took this action because “we want the company [Total] and the international community to know that the money the SPDC gets from the pipeline, they use to buy fighters, tanks, artillery, and by these weapons they fight our Karen people, they kill, rape, destroy our Karen people, they take people for forced labour and forced relocation, they oppress our people by many ways, and they oppress not only the Karen people but all the people of Burma. The Total company gas pipeline is very dangerous for our country and we want the world to know.” Mahn Sha said that another reason was that the KNU wishes to show the SPDC that although the Burma Army kills Karen people every day, “we are not afraid of their fighting. If they do not stop, we can fight them.” However, the KNU has also sent a message to the SPDC “to stop the offensive and make meaningful political dialogue”. But the SPDC has refused. “They don’t accept. Meaningful dialogue cannot happen in Burma. It is the responsibility of the SPDC. We are ready for dialogue. But the SPDC don’t accept.” Thai Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra’s offer to mediate between the SPDC and the ethnic groups (see CSW Report, Visit to the Thai-Burma Border, March 2-7 2003) has been refused by the SPDC.

Mahn Sha emphasised that despite the KNU’s attack on the gas pipeline, “we are not terrorists”. The KNU does not and will not “fight the people, attack hospitals, primary schools – we don’t fight them. We only fight the military regime.”

 

May 1st-2nd – Attended training programme led by Free Burma Rangers on behalf of the National Democratic Front (NDF) in a KNU meeting place inside Burma – led one session on human rights reporting.

 

May 3rd – Meeting with Zing Cung, General-Secretary of the National Democratic Front (NDF) and Vice-Chairman of the Chin National Front (CNF)

Zing Cung is the grandson of Rev. Sang Fen, the first Chin convert to Christianity. Rev. Sang Fen was the first President of the Zomi (Chin) Baptist Convention. He graduated in 1983 from Rangoon University and after 1988 joined the movement for democracy in Burma. In January 1989 he joined the Chin National Front (CNF), which had been established in 1988 in order to fight for democracy, a democratic federal union of Burma, and the right to self-determination for the ethnic nationalities. That struggle, however, had been going on since the Panglong Agreement in 1947, in which the Constitution which had been agreed required the formation of a federal union which granted equal status to the ethnic nationalities. That was never fulfilled, and efforts to resolve the issue peacefully failed. When Ne Win seized power in a coup in 1962, he opposed federalism, claiming it would break-up the union. “We were betrayed by the Burmans,” Zing Cung said.

In 1994, the Burmese regime offered to talk with the Chin. However the regime set a pre-condition which was unacceptable to the Chin: they said they would talk about economic and rural area development issues only, and not include political issues in the talks. Between 1994-2001 the regime approached the Chin four times, but never with any willingness to negotiate the political issues.

“We believe in a multi-ethnic country in which every nationality has equal opportunities and rights. That is the only way there will be peace,” Zing Cung said. The Chin are engaged in armed struggle, he explained, because they regard the Burma Army as a “foreign invading force” and “in order to survive we have to keep arms to protect ourselves”. But the Burma Army has responded excessively. Although the scale of human rights violations in Chin state may not be as large as in Karen, Karenni or Shan states, the same kind of human rights abuses take place on a “daily basis”: forced relocation, forced labour, religious persecution, rape, extrajudicial killings. There are 50,000 Chin refugees in India and more in other countries.

A significant issue is the SPDC’s policy of “Burmanisation”. Zing Cung said: “When I was young I learned the Chin language up to 4th Grade. But now there is no more chance for Chin children to learn their own language. In the school curriculum, the Chin language is never mentioned. Even religious literature is strictly censored. There is no chance to publish a Chin Bible in Burma – it has to be published in India and smuggled in. Even a Burmese Bible cannot be freely circulated. People with [large quantities of] Bibles are arrested.” The Chin are estimated to be 90% Christian. “Religious persecution happens on a daily basis.” Religious gatherings are often prohibited, and pastors and priests who are preaching in church often have to submit their sermons to the Burmese authorities for approval beforehand. In every major town or village, in the late 1980s and 1990s, the Chin people placed a cross as a symbol of their faith, but most of these have been destroyed by the SPDC and replaced with a Buddha statue.

Zing Cung regards this “Burmanisation” as “cultural genocide”. The SPDC, he believes, is trying to destroy Chin culture and tradition. “It is a systematic campaign by the Burmese military regime. When people lose their culture and traditions, they lose what they are campaigning for.” The SPDC changed the official name of the country from “Burma” to “Myanmar”. Superficially, “Myanmar” is a more equitable name. “Burma” is the old colonial name, and represents the Burman ethnic group. “Myanmar” includes all ethnic nationalities living in the union. But the spirit and political reality of a federal union of ethnic nationalities is not present. The Burmese language is enforced and Burmese culture is propagated through literature and media. Furthermore and most importantly, the regime which changed the name of the country is an illegitimate regime which does not represent the will of the people, and therefore it “does not have the right” to change the name.

The SPDC oppresses both Burman and non-Burman people, but the violations of human rights are more severe for the ethnic minorities. For the Burmans, there is no freedom of expression and association, but for the ethnic minorities their culture, language and national identity are being destroyed. “We are suffering all together, but we [the ethnic minorities] are suffering more,” Zing Cung said. For example, in Chin state fertile land has been taken away from Chin people, and Burmans from central Burma have been brought in, forcing the Chin people away.

Chin life expectancy is between 50 and 55. Poverty and hardship, forced labour and treatable diseases contribute to this low life expectancy. Furthermore, the SPDC harasses Chin people farming their land, and during harvest time sometimes forces Chin people to work for the army – thus destroying a whole year’s crops. The people, Zing Cung said, are suffering “physically and mentally”. “They are living in constant fear: fear of when the Burmese are going to knock on the door, take people to be porters, check who is visiting.”

The National Democratic Front (NDF) was formed on May 10th 1975 by ethnic minorities who share the same goals. There have been previous alliances since 1949, with different names. The NDF aims to build federalism with equal rights for the ethnic nationalities, and campaigns for the right to self-determination for every ethnic group. Their slogan is “With a united force we will achieve our victory.” There are currently eight members of the NDF, which includes the KNU, the Karenni National Progressive Party, and the CNF.

Since 1989 the SLORC/SPDC have signed ceasefires with some ethnic groups, notably the Kachin, the Wa and the Mon[6]. The terms of the ceasefire were attractive: the ethnic resistance groups were permitted to keep their arms, and work together for the economic and rural development of their land. These ceasefires had some impact on the effectiveness of the NDF, and some ceasefire groups withdrew from NDF. Only the New Mon State Party, which signed a ceasefire, remained in the NDF.

Zing Cung said that it is essential now to make the international community aware of the situation in Burma, and to increase diplomatic and economic pressure on the SPDC. “Very little is known in the world about the suffering of the ethnic people in Burma, the human rights violations, the refugees, the cultural genocide,” he said. The armed resistance groups are often dismissed by the international community because they are armed, he noted, but, he explained, “if we don’t hold arms we will not be able to organise or protect our people”. The strategy, therefore, has to involve three approaches: armed struggle, economic pressure and diplomatic pressure. The goal is tripartite dialogue between the SPDC, the NLD and the ethnic groups. “The NDF is always ready to have dialogue with the regime,” he said.

“Now is a critical point,” said Zing Cung. More serious action must be taken. “The United Nations has passed resolutions every year since 1994, calling for tripartite dialogue and condemning human rights abuses, but it has taken no significant steps. The UN and the international community must take stronger measures.” One problem is the differences in approach between the European Union (EU) and the Association of South East Asian Nations (ASEAN). The EU has taken a strong stand with sanctions, but ASEAN, especially China and India, have practised “constructive engagement”. There needs to be consistency. “The international community needs one voice, a voice for diplomatic pressure and sanctions.”

 

Meeting with Saw Ywa Hay, President of the Karen Youth Organisation (KYO)

We discussed the work of the KYO, which is involved in community development, health education and human rights documentation in IDP areas inside Karen state, and in youth work in the refugee camps in Thailand. The KYO would welcome volunteer teachers and trainers to provide English language classes, management and leadership training, and medical training, on a short or longer-term basis. The KYO also needs funding for its missions to the IDPs, to cover costs of transportation, medical supplies, video camera and film to document the situation, educational materials, spiritual materials including Bibles and other Christian texts, and other supplies. CSW provided funds for a health education booklet, and for supplies to IDPs (see “Aid” section above).

 

May 4th – Dr Martin Panter (CSW Australia) and Anthony Peel (CSW UK) arrive in Maesot

 

Visit to Mae Tao Clinic to meet with Aye Lwin, ABSDF Chief Medical Officer

Aye Lwin is a co-ordinator of Dr Cynthia Maung’s backpack medical teams, which now number 70. We discussed the medical situation inside Burma, and the work of the backpack medical teams. We then visited ABSDF landmine victims, for consultation with Mr Peel (surgeon) and Dr Panter (general practitioner) about their amputations and possible prosthesis.

 

May 5th – Dr Panter and Mr Peel depart for Karen areas to conduct medical training

 

Meeting with Colonel Nerdah Mya, Karen Foreign Minister and KNLA commander

Nerdah Mya expressed concern that the international community, particularly the UN and the United States, is not doing enough to put pressure on the SPDC. Part of the problem, he said, is the veto power of countries in the UN which are sympathetic to the Burmese regime, particularly China. ASEAN countries have not shown willingness to use their influence with the SPDC to push the regime towards reform; indeed, “everybody wants good [business] relationships with the SPDC so they don’t say anything, they keep quiet”. But, he noted, this is a regime which loots, rapes and kills and takes children as soldiers and villagers for forced labour. He said the Karen do not want this war, but they have to bear arms in order to defend themselves. While the SPDC has forced many children into the Burma Army, the KNLA does not forcibly conscript children. Those children in the KNLA are generally not sent to the frontline[7], he said, and instead are given education and work in the military base camps, whereas the SPDC sends children to the frontline.  Nerdah said that the Burma Army forces children to become soldiers because it wants to “enlarge its military strength”, increasing the size of the military from the current 300,000 to a target of 500,000. Many Burma Army soldiers are deliberately given amphetamine drugs by the SPDC, and therefore are uncontrolled when they attack villages and loots, burn, rape and kill. Burmese soldiers, according to Nerdah, are poorly trained, poorly equipped and poorly disciplined and “when given the opportunity to loot, they loot”. The Shan Womens Action Network published a report on the use of rape by the SPDC entitled License to Rape last year. Nerdah said that rape also takes place in Karen areas, but for Karen women “it is very shameful – when you are raped nobody will want to marry you” – so Karen rape victims do not dare to speak out.

 

Meeting with General Bo Mya, former KNU President, now Vice-President and Defence Minister

We discussed the history of the Karen struggle. General Bo Mya said that the Karen struggle is not only a struggle for democracy in Burma, but a struggle for the freedom of the Karen people, and the right to self-determination. “When the British were in Burma we worked hand in hand. I hope the British will think about our past history and will have a heart for the Karen people.”

 

6th May – Meeting with Saw Ba Thin Sein, President of the KNU

Saw Ba Thin said the Karen were struggling for freedom, democracy, equal rights and the right to self-determination. He emphasised the need for freedom of religion in Burma. The majority Burman population are predominantly Buddhist, but there are many Christians, especially among the ethnic minorities. “If there is no freedom, no democracy, then there is no chance for freedom of worship,” he said. He acknowledged that the Karen have suffered internal splits in the past, and said the way to prevent future splits is to educate Karen people politically. “To be a free nation, to gain freedom, people need to be aware of the situation politically,” he said. At the moment illiteracy is very high and there are “very few intellectuals” among the Karen. “We need to know about Burma, about Burman people, and about how to gain their sympathy and understanding.” The Karen struggle cannot be won by armed resistance alone, he said. “Simply fighting will not work. It is a political problem. It is impossible to win the war by armed struggle alone.” Saw Ba Thin also said that raising international awareness is essential, but it is difficult for Karen to do this themselves. He asked human rights groups like CSW to “tell people about the Karen and our suffering, tell people that the Karen are not terrorists, that we struggle for freedom, equal rights, the right of self-determination, to establish a federal system suitable for a country like Burma.” In conclusion, he added, the Karen “are not warmongers” but are fighting in self-defence. “What we need is peace, peace with justice, equal rights, based on democracy.”

 

Visit to Mae Tao Clinic and meeting with Dr Cynthia Maung

The clinic now sees 150-200 patients a day. It has 120 staff including 5 or 6 volunteer doctors, and sends 70 teams of backpack medics into the conflict areas in Burma.

 

7th May – Visit KNLA base across the border inside Burma: Dr Panter and Mr Peel gave medical training to Karen soldiers and medics for one week.

 

8th May – Visit Rev. Dr Simon and the Bible School in Mae La refugee camp

The KKBC Bible School was founded in 1984, and now has 233 students and 20 staff. The school runs a Bachelor of Theological Studies programme approved by the Baptist World Alliance and the Asian Baptist Federation, and a Bachelor of Arts programme in Political Science, History, Economy, Education and English. The medium for teaching is English. The school has a need now for approximately 30 Oxford English Dictionaries.

Pastor Simon was awarded the Human Rights Award from the Baptist World Alliance in 2000, only the second recipient of this award. The previous winner in 1995 was former US President Jimmy Carter.

 

Meeting with Micah Rahly, former Karen soldier in the Second World War

Micah Rahly described his experiences fighting in the British Signals Corps against the Japanese in the Second World War. He said that Britain’s refusal to assist the Karen since the Second World War has been “a betrayal of trust”. The British “washed their hands” of the situation. “It is the betrayal [of the Karen] by the British that has caused this suffering.”

 

9th May – Visit to a Karen IDP village inside Burma

We crossed the border and visited a Karen village which has been attacked by the SPDC and DKBA several times in recent years. However, there has been no attack for the past year.

 

Visit to Noe Bo Bible School

Noe Bo Bible School sends teams of evangelists into Burma every week, often travelling through SPDC and DKBA-controlled areas.

 

10th May – travel to Mae Hong Son via Chiang Mai

 

11th May – Meeting with Khu Oo Reh, Joint General-Secretary, Karenni National Progressive Party (KNPP)

SPDC attacks on Karenni villages and people continued throughout April and the beginning of May, according to Khu Oo Reh. The SPDC reportedly has plans to re-start construction of the Mochi-Toungoo road, and an order has been given to villagers to be ready to provide labour to repair the road and to carry logs and ammunition. Loikaw township was recently ordered to provide bamboo for the construction of an SPDC army base. Battalion 429 based in Proso, and Light Infantry Battalion 72 based in Demoso have asked local people to provide bamboo and logs for construction of a military base. Villagers have been ordered to guard the electricity pylon from Lobeta to Demoso, and they have been warned that they will have to pay a fine if anything happens to the electricity supply, which provides power to Toungoo and Rangoon.

Khu Oo Reh provided CSW with copies of 19 letters from SPDC commanders to village leaders summoning them to meetings and extorting money, livestock, labour and building materials from them. One letter ordered Karenni villagers to act as informants to provide the SPDC with information on the movements of Karenni soldiers. But he said that in recent months the SPDC has tended to issue written orders only summoning village leaders to a meeting, and then issue verbal orders to supply materials and money. (Copies of these letters and translations are available from the CSW UK office).

The KNPP welcomed Thai Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra’s offer to mediate between the ethnic groups and the SPDC, and wrote to the Prime Minister to express their support. However, no progress has developed and the SPDC rejected the offer, arguing that it is an “internal affair” and therefore does not require outside mediation.

 

12th May – Visit to Meredith Nunu and the children from the CSW-supported orphanage

Although CSW was denied access to Camp 1 to visit the orphanage which we sponsor, Meredith Nunu and the children were able to walk to a location outside the camp to meet with us. We received an update on Meredith’s plans for the orphanage and met with some of the children.

 

 

7.      The Karenni

 

The population of Karenni State is 250,000-300,000. There are now two refugee camps in Thailand, Camp 1 which has 17,849 refugees and Camp 2 which has 3,515, totalling 21,364[8]. Remaining refugees from Section 7 of the old Camp 3 (now merged into the new Camp 1) will be relocated to Camp 1 on May 21st. These are mostly disabled people, victims of landmines. The population birth rate inside Karenni State is static, while in the camps the average birth rate increase is four per cent. In Burma, the Burman average birth rate increase is 2.8%.

CSW heard reports that Burman soldiers are offered the incentive of a bag of rice worth 6,000 kyats if they marry a Karenni Christian woman. This is part of a deliberate effort to ‘Burmanise’ the population and destroy the ethnic minorities’ religion and culture. CSW also heard of a case where one woman in an SPDC-controlled relocation (or concentration) camp was raped on six occasions, and gave birth to six children – all now fatherless and a result of rape. Furthermore, CSW heard reports and discussed with eye witnesses the details of attacks on Karenni villages. In one attack, SPDC soldiers deliberately targeted a visiting medical team. Karenni people told CSW that whenever the SPDC find medicines available in Karenni villages, they either destroy them or take them for themselves, thus deliberately denying Karenni people access to vital medical care.

It should also be noted that restrictions on access to Karenni Camp 1 and 2 appear to be increasingly tight. CSW, a sponsor of an orphanage in Camp 1, was denied permission to visit the camp by the Thai check-point. CSW then approached the Immigration Office in Mae Hong Son to seek permission for future visits. The Immigration Office referred CSW to the District Office, which in turn referred us to the Governor’s Office. Staff at the Governor’s Office were unable to help.

8.     Child soldiers

 

CSW interviewed three former Burmese child soldiers who escaped from the Burma Army and are now being cared for by resistance groups. In November 2002, CSW also interviewed another former child soldier, Kyow Zeya (see CSW Report, Visit to the Thai-Burmese border, November 2002). These interviews are consistent with other reports of the use of child soldiers in Burma, notably Human Rights Watch’s report published this year called My Gun Was as Tall as Me.

 

 

  1. Chit San Maung, aged 17 – interviewed 26th April 2003

 

Chit San Maung is now 17 years old. When he 14 years old he was on his way to school in Minglado township, Rangoon, when a Burma Army sergeant grabbed him and forced him to join the military. He was sent to a recruit centre, and from there to a training centre at Yamadin for four and a half months. He was then sent to Deebo, and from there to the front-line where he worked as a guard for about three months before defecting to the KNU, who handed him to ABSDF. The KNU and the ABSDF have treated him well, he said, since he defected.  During his time in the training school and near the front-line, he was beaten many times. On one occasion, he was beaten with sticks by nine men. He was beaten for being late for line-up or late for meals. He said that in any group of 250 new recruits/trainees in the Burma Army, at least 100 were children. In the Burma Army, he said, his feeling was “not open” but in the refugee camp he feels “open”. He is now attending school, where his favourite subject is history.

 

 

  1. Yang Naing, aged 14 – (army identity number available)  – interviewed 6th May, 2003

 

Yang Naing was forced to join the Burma Army on 30th August, 2002. He was in 9th Grade at school, and was coming home from a special tutorial class at 9pm in Insein area, Rangoon. A policeman and a soldier grabbed him and took him to a military recruiting centre at Mingladaw. He spent three days in the recruiting centre and was then taken to a training centre where he spent four and a half months. He said some days he was well-treated, but other days he was badly treated. He was beaten several times with fists and sticks. Usually he was beaten because someone else in his section had done something wrong, so the whole section was punished. He said that there were 1,750 soldiers in the training centre, of whom 1,000 were children. After training, he was sent to Battalion 343 in Yee. He did not engage in combat, but worked as a guard and assisted military intelligence communications. However, he knew other children who were sent to the front-line to fight. In March this year he decided to escape, and told his superiors he was going down the mountain to collect firewood. He left the army because he could not stand the torture. After a day he met the KNU, and they took him in. They treated him well, he said. In Thailand in the care of the KNU, he said, “I can try to find my future. If I had stayed in the army, my future would be hopeless”. In the army he received a salary of 4,500 kyats. A basket of rice costs 10,000 kyats.

 

  1. Nay Myo Kyaw, aged 16 – army identity number: 257 345- interviewed 6th May, 2003

 

Nay Myo Kyaw was also forced to join the Burma Army on 30th August, 2002, in Daongwa, near Rangoon. He had gone to a tutorial class and at 6pm three soldiers pulled up alongside him in a car. “I was told I was being taken to serve as a soldier, and I was told to keep quiet and not ask questions,” he recalled. “When I said I did not want to be a soldier, they told me if I do not join the army, I would go to jail.” He spent three days at a recruiting centre for a medical check, and then underwent four months of training. Military training included how to set up and fire a gun, and how to behave in battle. On two occasions he was beaten with sticks and hands on his legs, thighs and the back of his knees. He was beaten so badly that he could not walk for a week. The reason he was beaten was that he had tripped over a box and slightly moved the box’s position on the ground. After four months training he was sent to Battalion 343 in Yee. He was not sent into combat, but had to collect logs and assist the military intelligence communications. He spent three weeks in the battalion. He decided to escape because “I could not bear the torture any more”. He and Yang Naing fled together (see Yang Naing’s account above). When the KNU found them, because Yang Naing looks young and is only 14, they took him immediately to a safe-house in Thailand. But Nay Myo Kyaw looked older and so they kept him in a cell in a KNU camp for a few days. He said the KNU treated him very well and did not beat him. When he was in the Burma Army, he had been told that if he joined the KNU, the KNU would kill him. The KNU kill many people, they told him. But he did not believe the propaganda and was not afraid when he met the KNU. He said that staying in Thailand was “much better” than being in the SPDC Army. “If I stayed in the army, I could get malaria, I could be shot in a KNU attack, and I could die,” he said. He wants the world to put pressure on the regime “to stop using forced child soldiers” and he wants child soldiers currently in the Burma Army “to flee and be free to study”. He said the reason the SPDC takes children to be soldiers is that it wants to increase the size of its force, but older soldiers are leaving so it needs to replace them. Younger soldiers are also easier to train, he said.

 

9.      Conclusions and Recommendations

 

The evidence provided in this report, both direct and anecdotal, contains nothing new. For several years reports have emerged from Burma of the widespread use of child soldiers, rape, forced labour, forced relocation, killing, looting and destruction of villages. There is evidence of religious persecution, particularly of Christians in Karen, Karenni and Chin states and of the Rohingya Muslims in Arakan state. Churches and Christian symbols in Karen, Karenni and Chin states are often destroyed and sometimes turned into or replaced by Buddhist symbols. There have been reports of a concerted effort by the SPDC to destroy the food supplies, medical provisions and livelihoods of the ethnic minorities. Over a million people have been internally displaced by the SPDC, either held in relocation or concentration camps controlled by the Burma Army, or trapped in the jungles with little access to food or medicine. There are also credible reports claiming that SPDC soldiers are often drugged, causing them to carry out particularly brutal crimes. Such reports continue to this day. It is time for the international community to take action to bring an end to the suffering of the ethnic minorities and to restore democracy, freedom and justice to Burma. Will the world continue to sit by while millions of people suffer?

 

CSW urges the following action by the international community:

 

  1. The United Nations should consider ways of enforcing its own resolutions on human rights in Burma and significantly increase pressure on the SPDC to engage in meaningful tripartite dialogue with the National League for Democracy (NLD) and the ethnic groups;
  2. The United States of America should place Burma higher on the political agenda, and should examine ways of bringing about “regime change” in Burma;
  3. The US, United Kingdom, European Union and the UN should investigate Burma’s increasing militarization;
  4. The UN, EU, US and UK should put pressure on the Association of South East Asian Nations (ASEAN) to end its policy of “constructive engagement” and non-interference, and use its influence with the SPDC to bring about pressure for change;
  5. China and India should be urged to use their influence with the SPDC to persuade the junta to end the use of child soldiers, forced labour, rape, killing, looting and other widespread violations of human rights, and engage in meaningful tripartite dialogue

 

Thailand

CSW expresses its appreciation to the Government of the Kingdom of Thailand for providing sanctuary to many Karen and Karenni over many years. However, CSW would urge the Thai authorities to continue to extend their generosity until such time as Burma is genuinely free and peaceful and the refugees can safely return to their country without fear of persecution, and to ease restrictions on access to the refugee camps for foreign visitors who desire simply to help and support those who are in need.

 

International aid

CSW expresses its deep concern about the reports that Britain has provided US$192,000 for a bat conservation study in Burma, whilst providing almost no cross-border assistance to IDPs. CSW urges the British Government to review this decision and to prioritise aid in the direction of those who are suffering most.

 

CSW is also concerned that the ABSDF no longer receives humanitarian support from USAID or the IRC. CSW would urge NGOs to review that decision and to consider providing food and medicines to those engaged in armed struggle. Whether or not one agrees with armed struggle, the people are human beings and need food and medicines.
